Background and Research

This page explains the research, motivation, and design reasoning behind Inclusive Play, focusing on why accessibility matters in gaming.

Project Overview

Inclusive Play explores accessibility in gaming through interactive simulations. The project is designed to raise awareness by allowing users to experience simplified examples of barriers that can affect gameplay.

Instead of only explaining accessibility, the website uses interaction to help users understand how design choices can affect visibility, control, hearing, and colour perception.

Personal Motivation

As someone who regularly plays games, I had not personally needed to rely on accessibility features before starting this project. Because of this, I initially saw them as useful additions rather than essential parts of design.

Through research, I began to understand how many players depend on features such as subtitles, colour-blind modes, scalable text, and alternative controls. This changed how I viewed accessibility and showed me how easily games can exclude users when these needs are ignored.

This was especially clear in smaller web-based games, where accessibility features are often limited or missing. Inclusive Play was created to explore these issues technically while also raising awareness of their importance.

Accessibility in Gaming

Accessibility in gaming means designing games so they can be used by as many people as possible, including players with visual, motor, hearing, or cognitive impairments.

👁️ Visual Barriers

Small text, poor contrast, blur effects, and cluttered layouts can make important game information difficult to see.

🎨 Colour Barriers

Games that rely only on colour can exclude users with colour vision deficiencies. Labels, icons, and patterns can make information clearer.

⌨️ Motor Barriers

Fast reactions, precise mouse movement, and fixed controls can create difficulty for users with reduced motor control.

🔇 Hearing Barriers

Audio-only cues can exclude deaf or hard-of-hearing players. Subtitles, captions, and visual feedback can reduce this barrier.

Design Rationale

The design of this project is influenced by inclusive design principles. These focus on reducing barriers instead of expecting users to adapt to inaccessible systems.

Each simulation highlights a specific accessibility issue found in games. The aim is not to fully replicate real-world disability experiences, but to encourage reflection and show why inclusive features matter.

Practical Accessibility

Features such as scalable text, themes, and colour-blind modes support usability.

Awareness Through Play

Simulations help users understand accessibility barriers through interaction.

Inclusive Design

Good design should consider different users from the beginning of development.
